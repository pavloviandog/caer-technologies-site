Phishing Emails: The #1 Way Hackers Get In

📅 May 2025 ⏱ 5 min read 🔒 Security 👤 Beginner Friendly

What Is a Phishing Email?

A phishing email is a fake message designed to look real — like it came from your bank, Microsoft, the IRS, or even a coworker. The goal is simple: trick you into clicking a link, entering a password, or opening an attachment that hands over access to your accounts or computer.

The name comes from "fishing" — hackers cast a wide net and wait for someone to bite. And it works. According to the FBI's Internet Crime Report, phishing is the most common cybercrime reported in the United States, affecting hundreds of thousands of businesses every year.

Hill Country Reality: Small businesses are targeted just as often as large corporations — sometimes more, because hackers assume smaller operations have fewer defenses.

How Does It Work?

Here's how a typical phishing attack plays out for a small business owner:

  1. You get an email that looks like it's from Microsoft, QuickBooks, your bank, or even a vendor you know. The logo looks right. The sender name looks right.
  2. The email creates urgency — "Your account will be suspended," "Unusual sign-in detected," or "Invoice attached for your review."
  3. You click the link and land on a fake website that looks identical to the real one. You type in your username and password.
  4. The hacker now has your credentials and can log in to your real account — email, banking, payroll, whatever the fake site was impersonating.

Some phishing emails skip the fake website entirely and just contain a malicious attachment — a Word doc or PDF that installs malware the moment you open it.

How Does This Hurt Your Business?

Real damage phishing causes: unauthorized wire transfers, stolen customer data, ransomware infections, locked email accounts, and HIPAA violations for healthcare businesses — all from one employee clicking one link.

Beyond the immediate financial hit, a successful phishing attack can:

  • Lock you out of your own email or Microsoft 365 account
  • Give attackers access to your entire contact list — which they use to phish your customers and vendors
  • Lead to a ransomware infection that shuts your whole operation down
  • Trigger regulatory fines if customer or patient data is exposed
  • Damage your reputation with clients who trusted you with their information

The IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report found the average cost of a breach caused by phishing is over $4.9 million — but even at a fraction of that scale, it can be devastating for a small Hill Country business.

How Do You Spot One?

Phishing emails have gotten very convincing, but these red flags still show up:

  • Urgent or threatening language — "Act now," "Your account will be closed," "Immediate action required"
  • Mismatched sender address — The name says "Microsoft Support" but the email is from a random Gmail address
  • Hover before you click — Hover your mouse over any link and look at where it actually goes in the bottom of your browser
  • Unexpected attachments — Especially .zip, .exe, or Office files you weren't expecting
  • Requests for login credentials or payment — Legitimate companies never ask for your password by email

For a deeper look at current phishing tactics, the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) maintains an up-to-date phishing guidance page.
